When a small group of living organisms (a small population) is separated from the main population from which they originated (for example, if they cross a mountain range or river, or if they move to another island and thus cannot easily return), they often find themselves in a different environment than they were before.
In this new environment, there are different resources and different rivals, so the new species will need to develop new traits or become a stronger rival than it was before.
The native population has not changed at all - they still require the same adaptation as before.
Over time, as the new population begins to adapt to their new environment, they start to look less and less like the other population.
In the end, after thousands or even millions of years, these two populations will look so different that they cannot be called the same species.
This process is called speciation, which means the formation of new species. Speciation is an inevitable consequence and an extremely important component of evolution.
