When a small group of living beings (a small population) is separated from the main population from which they originated (for example, if they move along a mountain range or river or relocate to a new island, making it difficult to move back), they often end up in a different environment than the one they were in before.
This new environment has different resources and various competitors, so the new population will need different traits or adaptations to be stronger competitors than they previously needed to be.
The initial population has not changed at all; they still need the same adaptation as before.
Over time, as the new residents begin to adapt to their new environment, they become increasingly less similar to the other residents.
After thousands or millions of years, both populations will look so different that they can no longer be called a single species.
We call this process speciation, which simply means the formation of new species. Speciation is an inevitable consequence and a very important component of evolution.
