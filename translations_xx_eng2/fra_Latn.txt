When a small group of living beings (a small population) is separated from the main population from which it originates (for example, if it moves over a mountain range or a river, or if it moves to a new island such that it cannot easily return), it often finds itself in a different environment from the one it was in previously.
This new environment has different resources and competitors, so the new population will need new characteristics or adaptations to be a strong competitor compared to what it needed before.
The original population has not changed at all; it still needs the same adaptations.
Over time, as the new population adapts to its new environment, it begins to resemble the other population less and less.
Finally, after thousands or even millions of years, the two populations will appear so different that they can no longer be considered as belonging to the same species.
We call this process "speciation," which simply means the formation of new species. Speciation is an inevitable consequence and a very important part of evolution.
